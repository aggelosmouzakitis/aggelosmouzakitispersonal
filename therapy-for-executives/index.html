Therapy for executives who are tired of performing for everyone except themselves

The problem

You are good at your job. The results confirm it, the title confirms it, the comp confirms it. But somewhere between the last promotion and the next board meeting, something shifted. The work still gets done. But the cost of producing it has changed.

Maybe it is the isolation that comes with seniority. Maybe it is an identity that has become so fused with performance that you do not know who you are without it. Maybe it is the feeling that you have built something impressive and still feel like you are one mistake away from it all falling apart.

These are not problems that another offsite, another coach, or another framework will solve. This is therapy territory.

What executive therapy is

Executive therapy is psychotherapy designed for people in high-stakes professional roles. Not a watered-down version of clinical work. Not coaching with a therapy label. Real therapeutic work that takes your professional context seriously instead of treating it as background noise.

It addresses the patterns underneath the performance: why your identity has become inseparable from your output, why success does not feel like enough, why certain dynamics at work keep repeating in ways that feel personal, because they are.

The difference between an executive therapist and a general therapist is context. Most therapists have never had to defend a pricing decision, manage a team through layoffs, or sit in a room where someone's career depends on what you say next. If your therapist needs 20 minutes of every session just to understand what happened in your week, that is time you are not spending on what actually matters.

Common questions

What is executive therapy, exactly?

Psychotherapy for people in leadership and senior roles. It goes beyond performance optimization into the patterns, histories, and internal dynamics that shape how you lead, relate, and make decisions. It requires a therapist who understands both the clinical work and the professional environment you operate in.

How is this different from executive coaching?

Coaching focuses on skills and strategy. Therapy works with what is underneath: why you are stuck, why certain patterns keep repeating, why the thing that used to work no longer does. The coaching industry has a credibility problem precisely because much of what gets labeled coaching actually requires therapeutic training to address properly.

I am not sure if I need therapy or coaching. How do I decide?

If the challenge is situational and skill-based, coaching is likely enough. If the same patterns keep showing up across roles, relationships, and decisions, and you suspect the real obstacle is internal, that is therapy work. Many executives benefit from both. Starting with therapy often makes coaching more effective afterward.

Why would a tech growth professional also be a therapist?

Because the gap between good therapy and effective therapy for executives is context. I have been on the other side of the table. I have managed the targets, the teams, the investors, the politics. That background is not a side note. It is the reason the therapeutic work lands differently.

Can smart, analytical people even benefit from therapy?

Yes, but the analytical mind is often the obstacle, not the tool. Self-analysis can become a meta way to maintain control rather than a path to change. Therapy with someone who understands that pattern is specifically designed to work with it, not get fooled by it.
