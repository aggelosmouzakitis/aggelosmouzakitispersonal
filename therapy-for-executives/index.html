Therapy for executives who are tired of performing for everyone except themselves

The problem

You are good at your job. You know that. But somewhere along the way the cost of doing it well started to change, and no amount of delegation, time off, or strategy adjustment seems to touch it. The work gets done, the results are there, but something underneath has shifted.

For some people it is the isolation that comes with seniority. For others it is an identity that has slowly become inseparable from output, to the point where slowing down feels dangerous. Or it is the persistent feeling of being one mistake away from losing everything you have built, even though the evidence says otherwise.

These are not problems that another offsite or another framework will solve. They tend to need a different kind of attention.

What executive therapy is

Executive therapy is psychotherapy for people in senior professional roles. Not coaching with a different label, and not a softer version of clinical work. It takes the professional context seriously rather than treating it as background noise, and it goes deeper than performance optimisation into the patterns that actually drive behaviour.

It looks at things like why identity becomes inseparable from output, why success does not settle the internal question it was supposed to answer, and why certain interpersonal dynamics at work keep repeating regardless of the company or the team.

The difference between an executive therapist and a general therapist is mostly context. A general therapist may be clinically competent but will spend weeks understanding what a product review is, why a board meeting is stressful, or what it means to carry a revenue target. That gap in context slows the work down and sometimes sends it in the wrong direction entirely.

How I work

Most of the executives I work with are dealing with some version of performance dependence: a pattern where achievement has become the primary way they regulate their sense of worth. That pattern can run for years without causing visible problems. When it starts to break down, what surfaces is burnout, anxiety, relationship strain, or a growing sense that something fundamental needs to change.

We work with the pattern underneath, not just the symptoms on top. I write about this in depth at Undisguised. The writing points at the patterns. The private work is where we move them.

Common questions

What makes executive therapy different from regular therapy?

Context. A general therapist may be clinically skilled but will not understand the specific pressures of senior leadership in tech. Executive therapy takes the professional environment seriously rather than treating it as background, and goes deeper than coaching into the patterns that actually drive behaviour.

What does executive therapy help with?

Common themes include chronic self-doubt despite strong performance, identity that has become inseparable from work, isolation from seniority, burnout that rest does not fix, and the disorientation of career transitions at senior levels.

Do I need therapy or coaching?

If the challenge is situational and skill-based, coaching is usually enough. If the same patterns keep repeating across different contexts, therapy is more likely to help. Many people benefit from both, but therapy often makes coaching more effective when it comes first.

Why would a growth professional also be a therapist?

Because understanding the professional environment my clients work in means we spend less time explaining context and more time on the actual work.
