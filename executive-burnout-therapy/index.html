You took the vacation. You came back feeling the same. The problem is not the workload.

Executive burnout

Everyone tells you to rest more, delegate more, set better boundaries. You have tried all of it. Maybe you even took a month off. And within two weeks of being back, the same weight returned, like it was waiting for you.

That is because what most people call burnout in executives is not about working too many hours. It is about the emotional cost of the work: chronic responsibility without support, constant performance of a version of yourself that takes effort to maintain, and an identity so fused with output that stopping feels like disappearing.

Rest does not fix this. Sometimes what feels like post-holiday anxiety is actually clarity about how unsustainable the current arrangement has become. That clarity is useful if you work with it instead of pushing it away.

What is actually happening

Executive burnout usually sits on top of deeper patterns. An inability to stop because your identity is enmeshed with constant output. A relationship with work where your value as a person depends on the next deliverable. Chronic overfunction that started long before this role, possibly long before your career.

The cynicism, the emotional flatness, the inability to care about things you used to care about: these are not character flaws. They are signals that the internal cost of the current arrangement has exceeded what you can sustain. And the typical advice (better time management, morning routines, meditation apps) does not touch the actual structure.

Many people build elaborate systems of "trying to change" that look productive but function as avoidance. Another book, another framework, another set of boundaries that lasts two weeks. If this sounds familiar, the problem is not willpower. It is that the pattern is serving a function that has not been addressed.

How therapy helps

Therapy for burnout works at the level of the pattern, not the symptoms. We look at what is driving the overwork: what it would mean to stop, what you are avoiding by staying in motion, why the idea of doing less feels dangerous instead of freeing.

I am a licensed psychotherapist who spent 12 years in B2B SaaS, including advising 50+ companies on growth. I understand the environment: the always-on culture, the ambiguity, the pressure to perform certainty when you feel none. That context is not background noise. It is central to why your burnout looks the way it does.

The goal is not to make you work less (though that might happen). The goal is to change your relationship to the work so that it costs less. Sometimes that means structural changes to the role. Sometimes it means something shifts internally and the same role becomes sustainable. The work clarifies which.

Common questions

Why does rest not fix my burnout?

Because the exhaustion is not caused by hours worked. It is caused by the emotional weight of the work: chronic responsibility, identity fusion with performance, and the inability to stop without feeling like you are failing. Rest addresses the symptom. Therapy works with the structure.

Is burnout a mental health condition?

The WHO classifies it as an occupational phenomenon. In practice, it often coexists with anxiety and depression. More importantly, executive burnout usually reveals older patterns around identity, control, and worth that therapy is specifically designed to address.

How do I know if I am burned out or just tired?

Tiredness resolves with rest. Burnout does not. If you have taken time off and come back feeling the same, the exhaustion is structural. Other signals: cynicism about work you used to care about, emotional flatness, difficulty with non-urgent decisions, and a persistent sense that something needs to change without being able to identify what.

Can I do therapy while still in the job?

Yes. Most clients are actively working during therapy. The goal is not to quit. It is to understand the patterns that are creating the exact problem you are trying to avoid and change your relationship to the work so the cost comes down.

I have tried coaching, meditation, and boundary-setting. Why didn't they work?

Because they address the surface. If the burnout is driven by a deep pattern, such as identity fusion with output, conditional self-worth, or a belief that hard work alone should be enough, managing symptoms does not change the source. Therapy goes to the level where the pattern was set up.
