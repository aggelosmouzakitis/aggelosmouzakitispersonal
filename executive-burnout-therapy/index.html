You took the vacation. You set the boundaries. You are still burned out. That is because the problem was never the hours.

The real problem

High-functioning burnout does not look like collapse. It looks like someone who is still performing, still delivering, still holding it together while something underneath is quietly running out. The exhaustion is not proportional to the workload. It is proportional to how much of your identity, your emotional regulation, and your sense of worth is running through your work.

That is performance dependence. When achievement becomes the primary way you feel okay about yourself, the question of who you are without it is not theoretical. It is the thing you are working to avoid every day. And it is the thing that makes burnout resistant to rest, boundaries, and time off.

Why the usual fixes fail

Coaching, boundary-setting, meditation, sabbaticals. These can help with situational overload. They do not help when the burnout is structural. If the source is a pattern where your worth depends on output, reducing the output does not fix the pattern. It just removes the thing that was holding you together, and what surfaces is worse: anxiety, emptiness, the feeling that without results you are nothing.

Hard work alone does not advance the way you think it does. And the belief that it should is often part of what keeps the cycle going. The burnout is not a sign that you need to work less. It is a sign that the relationship between your work and your sense of self needs to change.

Common questions

Is burnout a clinical condition?

Burnout is recognised by the WHO as an occupational phenomenon but is not a standalone psychiatric diagnosis. In practice, it often coexists with anxiety and depression. More importantly, executive burnout usually reveals older patterns around identity, control, and worth that therapy is specifically designed to address.

How do I know if I am burned out or just tired?

Tiredness resolves with rest. Burnout does not. If you have taken time off and come back feeling the same, the exhaustion is structural. Other signals: cynicism about work you used to care about, emotional flatness, difficulty with decisions that are not urgent, and a persistent sense that something needs to change without being able to identify what.

Can I do therapy while still in the job?

Yes. Most clients are actively working during therapy. The goal is not to quit. It is to understand the patterns that are creating the exact problem you are trying to avoid and change your relationship to the work so the cost comes down.

I have tried coaching, meditation, and boundary-setting. Why did they not work?

Because they address the surface. If the burnout is driven by a deep pattern (identity fusion with output, conditional self-worth, a belief that hard work alone should be enough), managing symptoms does not change the source. Therapy goes to the level where the pattern was set up.
