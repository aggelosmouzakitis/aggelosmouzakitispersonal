The next role is not the hard part. Figuring out who you are without this one is.

The real transition

You have spent years building a career that defines how other people see you and, more importantly, how you see yourself. Now something is shifting. Maybe you are considering leaving. Maybe you were pushed out. Maybe you made the move and expected relief but got disorientation instead.

The strategic questions (what industry, what role, what comp) are the easy part. Frameworks exist for those. The harder question is the one nobody around you is equipped to help with: who are you when the title, the team, and the daily structure that organized your identity are gone?

Most people think the grass is greener on the other side of the corporate/startup fence. They trade one set of friction for another and wonder why the relief was so short-lived. The problem was never the specific job. It was the relationship to work itself. And that travels with you.

What makes this hard

At senior levels, your career is not just what you do. It is the structure that holds your identity together. Your social life is built around it. Your daily rhythm depends on it. Your sense of competence is validated by it. When that structure changes, everything it was holding in place shifts too.

If you were laid off, the experience often produces a grief response that surprises you: not just about the job, but about the version of yourself that existed inside it. If you are choosing to leave, the paralysis is rarely about the options. The doubt is protecting you from something, usually the fear of what you will find (or not find) on the other side.

And if you made the move already and feel lost instead of liberated, that is not failure. It is the predictable result of removing a structure without understanding what it was compensating for.

Why therapy, not coaching

Career coaching helps you figure out what to do next. Therapy helps you understand why you are stuck, what the transition is really about, and what needs to shift internally for any external change to hold.

Without that internal work, people tend to recreate the same patterns in new contexts: the same overwork, the same identity fusion, the same conditional relationship with their own worth. They create the exact problem they were trying to leave behind.

I am a licensed psychotherapist who made this transition myself, from a 12-year career in B2B SaaS and growth advisory to clinical practice. I know what it costs to leave an identity that works. And I know what becomes possible when you do it with your eyes open instead of in a reactive panic.

Who this is for

Senior professionals considering a major career change but paralyzed by the decision. Executives who were laid off and are struggling with more than just the job search. Leaders who made the move and feel more lost than free. People who keep almost leaving but pull back every time. Anyone at a senior level who suspects the career question is actually a deeper question about identity, worth, and what they want from the second half of their working life.

Common questions

Why would I need therapy for a career transition?

Because at senior levels, a career change disrupts your identity, your social structure, and your sense of competence. The strategic part is rarely the problem. The harder part is who you are when the role that organized your life is gone. That is therapeutic work.

I cannot decide whether to leave. Can therapy help with that?

Yes. The paralysis is almost never about information. You usually know enough. The block comes from what the decision represents: loss of identity, fear of regret, the gap between what you want and what you think you should want. Endless pondering has its own cost, and therapy works with the internal conflict that makes the decision feel impossible.

Is this career coaching?

No. Coaching works on what to do next. Therapy works on why you are stuck, what the transition is really about, and what needs to shift inside you for any external change to last. Much of what gets labeled career coaching actually requires therapeutic depth to address properly.

I was laid off and I feel lost. Is this normal?

Completely. Involuntary exits at senior levels produce genuine grief, not just about the job, but about the identity and structure it provided. Most people around you will not understand this because they see it as "just a job." It is not. Therapy gives you a space to process the loss honestly before rushing into the next thing.

I already made the move and feel worse. What happened?

You removed the structure without understanding what it was compensating for. The old role was holding things in place, things like your sense of purpose, your daily identity, your social network, your feeling of mattering. Without it, those gaps become visible. This is not a sign you made the wrong choice. It is a sign there is deeper work to do, and now you have the space to do it.
