You know you need to leave. Or you already left. Either way, the hard part is not the strategy. It is who you are without the role.

The real difficulty

At senior levels, a career transition is not a job change. It is an identity disruption. The role was not just what you did. It was how you organised your days, your relationships, your sense of purpose, and your feeling of mattering. When that structure goes away, what surfaces is not freedom. It is the gap the role was filling.

Some people get stuck before the move: they know they need to leave but cannot bring themselves to do it. Others make the move and feel worse, not better. In both cases, the block is rarely strategic. It is emotional. The decision represents something about identity, worth, and belonging that the spreadsheet cannot capture.

What a career transition reveals

For people whose sense of self has been running through their professional performance for a long time, a career transition exposes the dependency. The question of who you are if you are not crushing it becomes unavoidable when the structure that let you avoid it is gone.

This is not a sign you made the wrong choice. It is a sign there is deeper work to do, and now you have the space to do it. The old role was holding things in place: your sense of purpose, your daily identity, your social network, your feeling of being enough. Without it, those gaps become visible.

How I work with this

I am a licensed psychotherapist who made this transition myself, from 18+ years in B2B SaaS (including advising 50+ companies on growth) to training as a therapist. Common questions

Why would I need therapy for a career transition?

At senior levels, a career change disrupts your identity, social world, and sense of competence. The strategic part is rarely the real difficulty. The harder part is who you are when the structure that organised your life is gone.

I cannot decide whether to leave. Can therapy help?

Usually yes. The indecision almost never comes from lack of information. The block tends to come from what the decision represents: loss of identity, fear of regret, the distance between what you want and what you think you should want.

Is this career coaching?

No. Coaching works on what to do next. Therapy works on why you are stuck and what needs to shift internally for any external change to hold.

I was laid off and feel lost. Is that normal?

Very. Involuntary exits at senior levels produce genuine grief about the identity and structure the role provided, not just the job itself.

I made the move and feel worse. What happened?

You probably removed the structure without understanding what it was compensating for. The gaps it was holding in place become visible. That is not a sign of a wrong choice. It is a sign there is deeper work to do, and now you have the space to do it.
