You have the track record. You have the title. And you still feel like you are about to be found out.

The pattern

You know you are competent. The evidence is there. But the evidence does not settle the question. Every new success buys temporary relief, never lasting security. You over-prepare. You avoid situations where you might look like you do not know what you are doing. You attribute wins to luck or timing and failures to something fundamental about you.

This is not a confidence problem. It is a pattern that was set up long before your career started, usually in a family environment where worth was conditional on performance. The career did not create it. The career gave it the perfect stage to run on.

Why the usual advice does not work

"Keep a wins folder." "Remind yourself of your accomplishments." "Just remember how far you have come." These approaches treat imposter syndrome as a thinking problem. It is not. It is a feeling problem with roots that predate your career by decades.

Self-analysis, for smart analytical people, often becomes another way to maintain control rather than a path to change. You end up understanding the pattern perfectly while remaining stuck inside it.

The doubt is real. The story it tells you about what it means is not. Therapy works with that distinction at a level that self-help and coaching cannot reach.

Common questions

Is imposter syndrome a real diagnosis?

It is not a clinical diagnosis in the DSM. It is a persistent pattern of doubting your accomplishments despite objective evidence of competence. In senior professionals, it drives overwork, avoidance of visibility, difficulty delegating, and chronic anxiety that erodes both performance and wellbeing.

Why is imposter syndrome so common in high achievers?

Because achievement often started as a strategy to earn love, approval, or safety. The pattern works, so it persists. But it never produces lasting internal security, which is why the doubt returns after every success.

Can therapy actually fix imposter syndrome?

Therapy does not fix it by making you more confident. It works by changing your relationship to the doubt, so that it no longer drives your decisions or defines your sense of competence. The doubt may still show up. It just stops running the show.

How is this different from coaching for imposter syndrome?

Coaching works with the current situation. Therapy works with the pattern underneath it, which usually predates your career by decades. If the same doubt keeps returning despite evidence and reassurance, the issue is not situational.
